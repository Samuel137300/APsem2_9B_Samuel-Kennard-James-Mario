Grade 9 Physics – Chapter 1: Static Electricity

Definition

Static electricity is the buildup of electric charges on the surface of an object. It happens when electrons move from one object to another.

Usually, this happens when two objects touch and then separate. One object may gain electrons while the other loses electrons.

Unlike current electricity, static electricity does not flow continuously. The charge stays on the object until it suddenly releases, such as a spark or lightning.

Interaction Between Charges

Electrons → Negative charge
Protons → Positive charge

Rules of electric charges:

  • Same charges repel each other (+ + or − −)
  • Opposite charges attract each other (+ −)

This is why charged objects can pull or push other objects.

Conductor

Materials that allow electrons to move easily from one particle to another.

Examples:

  • Copper
  • Aluminum
  • Iron

Insulator

Materials that do not allow electrons to move easily.

Examples:

  • Plastic
  • Rubber
  • Wood
  • Glass

Polarization

Polarization is the separation of positive and negative charges inside an object.

The object stays overall neutral, but the charges move to different sides.

Example: A charged rod brought near a neutral object can cause the charges inside the object to separate.

Charging Methods

Charging by Friction (Rubbing)

Two objects are rubbed together. Electrons move from one object to another. The two objects become oppositely charged.

Example: Rubbing a balloon on hair.

Charging by Conduction

Charging happens by direct contact. A charged object touches a neutral object, and electrons move between them.

Charging by Induction

Charging happens without touching the object. A charged object is brought near another object and causes the charges to rearrange inside the object.

Discharging (Grounding / Earthing)

Discharging happens when extra charge moves to the Earth.

The Earth can accept or give electrons, so it helps remove extra charges.

Example: Touching a charged metal object and feeling a small electric shock.

Electron Affinity

Electron affinity is a property that shows how strongly a material attracts electrons.

  • High electron affinity → easily gains electrons
  • Low electron affinity → does not gain electrons easily

Triboelectric Charging

Triboelectric charging happens when electrons move from one material to another during rubbing.

  • Material that loses electrons → becomes positively charged
  • Material that gains electrons → becomes negatively charged

This depends on the triboelectric series, which ranks materials based on how easily they gain or lose electrons.
